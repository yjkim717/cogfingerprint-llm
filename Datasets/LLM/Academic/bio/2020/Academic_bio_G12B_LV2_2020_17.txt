**Abstract**

The escalating demand for comprehensive healthcare services in India is increasingly constrained by a critical shortage of qualified allied health professionals (AHPs). This workforce deficit, observed across diverse clinical settings, significantly impacts healthcare delivery quality and patient outcomes. While physician availability has improved in recent decades, the proportional growth of AHPs – including, but not limited to, medical laboratory technicians, radiographers, physiotherapists, and occupational therapists – has failed to keep pace, creating a demonstrable imbalance within the healthcare ecosystem.  Current training pathways for AHPs are characterized by considerable variability in curricula, accreditation standards, and pedagogical approaches, leading to inconsistencies in professional competency and potentially compromising patient safety. This paper argues for the urgent establishment of a centralized national council dedicated to the governance and regulation of AHP education and practice. Such a council would facilitate the development and implementation of standardized curricula, promote evidence-based training methodologies, and establish rigorous accreditation processes.  Furthermore, a centralized body could strategically address workforce planning, proactively forecasting future needs and guiding the expansion of AHP training programs to align with national healthcare priorities. The implementation of a unified regulatory framework is posited to enhance the quality and quantity of the AHP workforce, thereby strengthening India’s overall healthcare infrastructure and improving access to essential services by 2020 and beyond.
