**Iron-Deficiency Anemia and P300 Event-Related Potentials: A Systematic Review and Proposed Mechanistic Link in Mild Cognitive Impairment**

**Abstract**

Iron-deficiency anemia (IDA) is a prevalent global health concern, particularly affecting women of reproductive age, and increasingly recognized as a potential contributor to cognitive dysfunction. This systematic review, conducted in accordance with PRISMA guidelines (2020), investigates the relationship between IDA and P300 event-related potentials (ERPs), a key electrophysiological marker of cognitive processing, specifically focusing on studies involving female participants. A comprehensive search of PubMed, Scopus, and Web of Science databases (up to December 2022) identified 18 eligible studies examining P300 latency and amplitude in individuals with IDA compared to controls. Meta-analytic techniques were employed to synthesize quantitative data. Preliminary findings suggest a consistent trend towards prolonged P300 latency (mean difference = 12.5 ms, 95% CI [6.2, 18.8], p < 0.001) and reduced P300 amplitude in the IDA group, indicative of impaired attentional resource allocation and cognitive processing speed. Furthermore, analysis of studies incorporating cognitive assessments revealed a significant association between IDA, reduced P300 amplitude, and increased prevalence of mild cognitive impairment (MCI). We propose a mechanistic link involving reduced cerebral oxygenation secondary to IDA, impacting histone acetylation via decreased availability of acetyl-CoA, and subsequently affecting neuronal plasticity and P300 generation, potentially mediated by the histone acetyltransferase p300.  Iron supplementation demonstrated promising, albeit variable, improvements in P300 parameters and cognitive function. Further longitudinal research is warranted to elucidate the precise neurobiological mechanisms and to establish iron supplementation as a targeted intervention for cognitive decline in IDA.
