**Abstract**

The efficient manipulation of fungal genomes remains a critical bottleneck in the advancement of industrial biotechnology, particularly concerning lignocellulosic biomass degradation. *Pleurotus ostreatus*, a widely utilized saprotrophic fungus, exhibits considerable potential for enzymatic breakdown of recalcitrant plant material, yet its application is often limited by inherent metabolic inefficiencies. Here, we report the development and validation of a novel genetic engineering strategy for generating targeted, multiple-gene mutations in *P. ostreatus*. Our approach leverages a polycistronic transfer RNA (tRNA) vector coupled with CRISPR/Cas9 guide RNA (gRNA) technology, enabling the simultaneous disruption of multiple genes within a single transformation event. This method circumvents the limitations of sequential gene editing, significantly accelerating the generation of mutant libraries required for metabolic engineering efforts.

Specifically, we demonstrate the successful implementation of this polycistronic CRISPR/Cas9 system to induce mutations in genes implicated in lignocellulose degradation pathways. Preliminary analysis of mutant strains confirms targeted gene disruption and assesses the resultant phenotypic impact on enzymatic activity, demonstrating a statistically significant alteration in the degradation profile. This innovative methodology represents a substantial improvement over existing techniques for fungal genome editing, offering a streamlined and highly effective route for optimizing *P. ostreatus* for enhanced lignocellulose utilization. The described system holds broad applicability for accelerating strain improvement across a range of filamentous fungi, furthering the development of sustainable bioprocesses.
