**Abstract**

Intracellular transport of endosomes is crucial for cellular homeostasis and signaling, yet the underlying dynamics often deviate from classical Brownian motion. This study investigates the spatiotemporal trajectories of both slow- and fast-moving endosomes within a eukaryotic cell, employing advanced statistical methods to characterize their anomalous diffusive behavior. We utilized local analysis techniques to estimate anomalous diffusion exponents and diffusion coefficients directly from individual trajectories, circumventing limitations associated with global fitting approaches. Our results demonstrate a statistically significant distinction in local anomalous properties between the two endosomal subpopulations. Specifically, slow-moving endosomes exhibited consistently lower anomalous exponents and diffusion coefficients compared to their faster counterparts. These observations are consistent with a heterogeneous model of intracellular transport, suggesting that endosomal movement is best described by fractional Brownian motion (fBm) with distinct Hurst exponents reflecting varying degrees of memory and long-range correlations. The observed heterogeneity challenges simplified diffusion models and highlights the importance of considering local, trajectory-dependent parameters to accurately characterize intracellular transport processes. Further investigation into the mechanisms governing these distinct anomalous behaviors is warranted to fully elucidate the regulatory landscape of endosomal trafficking.
