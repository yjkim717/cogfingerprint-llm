**Spatiotemporal Dynamics and Risk Assessment of Highly Pathogenic Avian Influenza (HPAI) H5N1 Outbreaks in the European Union and United Kingdom: A Late-2021 Surveillance Report**

**Abstract**

The emergence and rapid dissemination of Highly Pathogenic Avian Influenza (HPAI) viruses, specifically the H5N1 subtype, pose a significant and escalating threat to global poultry production and public health. This report presents a detailed analysis of HPAI H5N1 detections across 27 European Union (EU) and European Economic Area (EEA) countries, alongside the United Kingdom, spanning the period of September to December 2021.  A total of 867 confirmed HPAI virus detections were recorded during this four-month surveillance window, representing a substantial increase in reported cases compared to preceding years.  Geographic distribution analysis reveals clustered outbreaks primarily concentrated in wild bird populations, particularly migratory waterfowl, with subsequent spillover events impacting domestic poultry farms.  Molecular characterization, based on available sequence data, indicates the prevalence of reassortant H5N1 genotypes exhibiting enhanced virulence and transmissibility.  The observed spatiotemporal patterns suggest a complex interplay of factors, including migratory routes, climatic conditions, and varying levels of biosecurity implementation within poultry operations.  We employed a risk assessment framework, incorporating epidemiological data and existing knowledge of H5N1 biology, to evaluate the potential for further disease propagation and the impact on the EU/EEA poultry sector.  Results highlight critical vulnerabilities related to inadequate biosecurity practices on certain farms and insufficient surveillance capacity in specific regions.  This report underscores the urgent need for strengthened biosecurity measures, enhanced wild bird monitoring programs, and improved coordination of cross-border disease control strategies to mitigate the ongoing risk of HPAI H5N1 outbreaks and safeguard both poultry industries and public health within the EU/EEA and the UK.  Further research focusing on viral evolution and the effectiveness of different intervention strategies is warranted.
