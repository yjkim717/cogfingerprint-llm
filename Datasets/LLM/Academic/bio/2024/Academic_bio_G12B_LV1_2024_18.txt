**Abstract:**

The ongoing enzootic and sporadic epizootic events of highly pathogenic avian influenza (HPAI) across Europe, primarily driven by the H5N1 virus, pose a significant and evolving threat to poultry industries and wild bird populations. This study investigates the genomic landscape of H5N1 viruses circulating in Europe between 2020 and 2024, employing a phylodynamic analysis of whole-genome sequencing data obtained from diverse geographic locations. Our findings reveal a remarkable degree of genetic diversity within the prevalent clade 2.3.4.4b, characterized by a high mutation rate and punctuated by recurrent reassortment events. We identify several distinct lineages exhibiting unique evolutionary trajectories, highlighting the complex dynamics of viral spread and adaptation.

A key focus of this research is the identification and quantification of reassortment events between circulating H5N1 viruses and pre-existing avian influenza viruses, particularly those of low pathogenicity.  These reassortments appear to be a critical driver of phenotypic change, contributing to increased transmissibility and virulence. Furthermore, we observed evidence of positive selection pressures targeting viral surface glycoproteins, particularly hemagglutinin (HA) and neuraminidase (NA), suggesting adaptation to novel host species and/or immune evasion strategies.  We model the impact of these evolutionary processes on the emergence of epidemic waves, demonstrating a correlation between reassortment frequency and the intensity and geographic reach of outbreaks.  Finally, we discuss the implications of these findings for surveillance strategies and the development of effective mitigation measures, emphasizing the need for continuous genomic monitoring to anticipate and respond to the unpredictable evolution of HPAI viruses in Europe.
