**Thermodynamic Limits to Quantum Computation Scaling: A Fidelity-Constrained Analysis**

The realization of fault-tolerant quantum computation hinges on the ability to maintain qubits in coherent superpositions and entangled states for extended durations. Paradoxically, the stringent requirement of near-zero temperatures demanded by current quantum computing architectures presents a fundamental tension with the third law of thermodynamics. This work investigates the implications of this conflict for the scalability of quantum systems, arguing that achieving arbitrarily high fidelity in qubit operations while adhering to thermodynamic constraints poses a significant, and potentially limiting, challenge. We present a theoretical framework that explicitly couples qubit fidelity metrics with the energy dissipation necessary to maintain cryogenic temperatures, particularly focusing on the entropy generation associated with cooling processes. Our analysis reveals that strict fidelity constraints, essential for error correction and complex quantum algorithms, inherently necessitate increasingly sophisticated and energy-intensive cooling schemes. Consequently, we demonstrate that the thermodynamic cost of maintaining qubit coherence, as quantified by the power required for cooling, scales unfavorably with increasing qubit count and fidelity demands. These findings highlight the need for novel quantum architectures and cooling technologies that minimize thermodynamic overhead and potentially circumvent the conventional zero-temperature paradigm.
