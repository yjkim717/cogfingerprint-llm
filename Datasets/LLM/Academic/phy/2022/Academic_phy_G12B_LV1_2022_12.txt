**Abstract: Quantum-Classical Simulation of Rhodopsin Dynamics Reveals Excited-State Population Splitting and its Implications for Quantum Efficiency**

The remarkable quantum efficiency of vision – the ability of rhodopsin to convert a single photon into a substantial physiological response – has long posed a fundamental challenge to biophysical understanding. While the photoisomerization of retinal within the rhodopsin protein scaffold is the initiating event, the precise mechanisms underlying the high efficiency remain elusive. Here, we present results from a novel quantum-classical simulation framework applied to the early dynamics of photoexcited rhodopsin. Our simulations, employing a combination of time-dependent density functional theory (TD-DFT) for the chromophore and a coarse-grained protein environment, reveal the emergence of significant excited-state population splitting within the first picoseconds following photoexcitation. This splitting is attributed to subtle, yet crucial, interactions between the retinal chromophore and the surrounding protein residues, influencing the pathways available for subsequent double-bond isomerization.  Crucially, we demonstrate a direct correlation between the extent of this splitting and the predicted quantum efficiency of the photoreaction.  Furthermore, analysis incorporating thermal noise suggests that this phenomenon may be evolutionarily selected for, as even modest stabilization of one specific excited-state manifold can significantly enhance efficiency. These findings provide a novel perspective on the quantum underpinnings of vision, offering a potential link between microscopic dynamics, macroscopic performance, and the evolutionary pressures shaping biological light-harvesting systems. These insights contribute to the ongoing effort to reconcile quantum mechanical phenomena with the warm, wet environment of the biological world.
