**Dynamically Tunable Metasurfaces via Electrochemical Lithiation-Induced Hierarchical Disorder and Dielectric Polarization**

The realization of dynamically reconfigurable electromagnetic devices remains a significant challenge in nanophotonics. While existing approaches often rely on mechanical actuation or thermal control, we present a novel strategy leveraging electrochemical lithiation to achieve real-time modulation of metasurface properties. This work demonstrates a proof-of-concept platform utilizing a two-dimensional array of plasmonic resonators fabricated from a lithium-ion intercalatable material, specifically a layered transition metal dichalcogenide (TMD). Applying a controlled electrochemical potential induces reversible lithiation, leading to a hierarchical disordering process at the atomic scale within the TMD lattice. This disorder, characterized by the introduction of point defects and lattice distortions, fundamentally alters the local dielectric environment surrounding the plasmonic resonators.

Critically, we observe a concomitant and substantial change in the overall dielectric permittivity of the metasurface, driven by the redistribution of charge carriers and the emergence of polarization phenomena linked to the electrochemical reaction. Finite-difference time-domain (FDTD) simulations corroborate experimental observations, revealing a direct correlation between the lithiation level, the degree of disorder, and the resulting shift in resonant frequencies and transmission characteristics.  Furthermore, we demonstrate the potential for electromagnetic switching behavior through the application of pulsed voltage sequences. This approach bypasses the limitations of traditional tuning methods, offering opportunities for low-power, high-speed optical modulation, and potentially enabling novel functionalities in areas such as reconfigurable beam steering, dynamic filtering, and tunable sensing. Future research will focus on optimizing material selection, scaling up device dimensions, and exploring integration with active electronic components for advanced photonic integrated circuits.
