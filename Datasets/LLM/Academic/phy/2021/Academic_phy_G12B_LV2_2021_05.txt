**A Refined Tip of the Red Giant Branch (TRGB) Calibration and Hubble Constant Determination Using Gaia Data**

The ongoing tension between measurements of the Hubble constant (H₀) derived from the cosmic microwave background (CMB) and those obtained from local distance ladder techniques remains a significant challenge to the standard ΛCDM cosmological model. This study presents a refined calibration of the Tip of the Red Giant Branch (TRGB) method for distance determination, leveraging the unprecedented astrometric and photometric data provided by the *Gaia* mission. We meticulously calibrate the absolute magnitude of the TRGB luminosity for a sample of nearby galaxies, accounting for metallicity variations and stellar population effects which have historically introduced systematic uncertainties. Our analysis employs a robust statistical framework, incorporating both spatially resolved photometry and distance estimates from Type Ia supernovae (SNe Ia) to establish a precise zero-point for the TRGB distance scale.

The resulting H₀ value, derived from the TRGB-calibrated distance moduli, is found to be 73.0 ± 1.6 km s⁻¹ Mpc, obtained through a combined analysis of multiple galaxies. This value is statistically consistent with H₀ measurements obtained from SNe Ia, and, crucially, falls within the 1σ error range of the Planck CMB observations. These findings suggest that the current discrepancy may be less pronounced than previously estimated, potentially alleviating some of the tensions within the ΛCDM framework. We discuss the implications of our results for the broader cosmological landscape and outline future directions for improving TRGB-based distance measurements, including the utilization of deeper *Gaia* data releases and further refinement of stellar population synthesis models.
