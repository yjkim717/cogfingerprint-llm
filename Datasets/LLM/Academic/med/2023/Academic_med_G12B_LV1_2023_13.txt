**Abstract**

Previous risk assessments primarily focused on endocrine disruption, however, accumulating *in vitro* and *in vivo* data suggested a more nuanced and potentially concerning role for BPA in immune system dysregulation.

Analysis of available toxicological data revealed a lower benchmark dose associated with statistically significant alterations in Th17 cell cytokine production and subsequent modulation of inflammatory responses. This finding indicates that exposure levels previously considered safe may contribute to subtle, yet potentially cumulative, immune system perturbations.  The revised TDI reflects these findings, aiming to mitigate the risk of BPA-induced shifts in Th17 cell activity and their downstream effects on systemic immunity.

Furthermore, the re-evaluation considered the potential for BPA to exacerbate pre-existing inflammatory conditions and influence susceptibility to infectious diseases. While definitive causal links remain under investigation, the precautionary principle guided the reduction of the TDI to ensure a wider margin of safety, particularly for vulnerable populations such as pregnant women and young children.  Continued research is warranted to further elucidate the mechanisms by which BPA impacts Th17 cell differentiation and function, and to assess the long-term health consequences of low-level BPA exposure.
