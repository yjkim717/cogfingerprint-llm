**Abstract**

Pulmonary hamartomas are typically asymptomatic, incidental findings detected via radiological imaging. This case report describes an atypical presentation of a pulmonary hamartoma in a 44-year-old female patient who presented with persistent cough and intermittent chest discomfort. Diagnostic workup, including computed tomography (CT) scanning, confirmed a solitary pulmonary nodule with characteristic hamartomatous features. While surgical resection remains the gold standard for definitive diagnosis and treatment, the patient’s symptomatic presentation and concerns regarding surgical morbidity prompted a strategy of close observation with serial CT imaging.  This approach allowed for monitoring of nodule stability over a six-month period.  The case highlights the uncommon occurrence of symptomatic pulmonary hamartomas and underscores the importance of individualized management strategies.  Furthermore, it demonstrates that, in select cases, conservative surveillance with advanced imaging techniques can be a viable alternative to immediate surgical intervention, particularly when considering patient-specific risk factors and symptom burden. Further research is warranted to define optimal surveillance protocols for symptomatic pulmonary hamartomas.
