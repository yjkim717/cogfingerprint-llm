**Genetic Evidence for a Protective Role of Earlier Chronotype on Major Depressive Disorder Risk: A Mendelian Randomization Study**

**Background:** Major Depressive Disorder (MDD) represents a significant global health burden, with complex etiology involving both environmental and genetic factors. Disrupted circadian rhythms and sleep disturbances are consistently implicated in MDD pathogenesis, suggesting a potential mechanistic link. Diurnal preference, or chronotype – an individual's propensity to sleep and wake at specific times – is a key component of circadian regulation. While observational studies have suggested an association between later chronotypes (eveningness) and increased MDD risk, the direction of causality remains unclear due to potential confounding and reverse causation.  Mendelian randomization (MR) offers a robust approach to inferring causal relationships by leveraging genetic variation as instrumental variables.

**Methods:** This study employed a two-sample MR design to investigate the causal relationship between genetically proxied diurnal preference and MDD risk. We utilized single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) previously identified in genome-wide association studies (GWAS) of diurnal preference (sleep midpoint measured in minutes from midnight) in a European ancestry cohort (N = 37,717) as instrumental variables.  The outcome data were obtained from a large-scale GWAS of MDD (N = 83,749), also of European ancestry.  MR analyses were performed using an inverse variance weighted (IVW) method as the primary approach, supplemented by MR-Egger regression and weighted median estimation to assess the robustness of the findings and detect potential horizontal pleiotropy. We further explored potential non-linear effects by examining the association between genetically predicted sleep midpoint deviations and MDD risk in quintiles.  Sensitivity analyses were conducted to assess the impact of genetic variants associated with intermediate phenotypes (e.g., melatonin secretion, cortisol rhythms) that could confound the observed association.

**Results:** The IVW analysis revealed a significant inverse association between genetically proxied earlier sleep midpoint (i.e., earlier diurnal preference) and MDD risk (β = -0.18, 95% CI: -0.29, -0.07, p = 0.001).  MR-Egger regression and weighted median estimates yielded consistent results, suggesting minimal evidence of directional pleiotropy.  The non-linear analysis indicated a graded inverse relationship, with individuals genetically predisposed to earlier sleep midpoints across all quintiles exhibiting a reduced risk of MDD compared to those in the later sleep midpoint quintile (p < 0.0001). Sensitivity analyses, while revealing some evidence of potential confounding by melatonin rhythm, did not substantially alter the overall direction or magnitude of the effect.
