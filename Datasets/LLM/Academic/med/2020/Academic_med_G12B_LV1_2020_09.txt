**Abstract**

**Genetic Predisposition to Daytime Napping and its Association with Cardiometabolic Risk: A Genome-Wide Mendelian Randomization Analysis**

Background: The prevalence of daytime napping varies considerably across populations, and its relationship with cardiometabolic health remains incompletely understood. While observational studies have yielded conflicting results, potential confounding factors limit their ability to establish causality. This study aimed to identify genetic variants associated with daytime napping frequency and to investigate whether these variants provide evidence for a causal relationship between napping and indicators of cardiometabolic health.

Methods: Utilizing data from the UK Biobank (n=335,576), we performed a genome-wide association study (GWAS) to identify single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) significantly associated with self-reported napping frequency (number of naps per week). Subsequently, we employed Mendelian randomization (MR) analyses, utilizing these instrumental SNPs as proxies for napping, to assess the potential causal effects of napping on several cardiometabolic outcomes, including body mass index (BMI), systolic blood pressure (SBP), diastolic blood pressure (DBP), fasting glucose, and high-density lipoprotein cholesterol (HDL-C).  MR-Egger regression, weighted median estimation, and MR-PRESSO were employed to assess and mitigate potential pleiotropic effects.

Results: The GWAS identified 96 independent SNPs reaching genome-wide significance (p < 5 x 10<sup>-8</sup>) associated with napping frequency. MR analyses demonstrated a statistically significant inverse association between genetically predicted napping frequency and BMI (β = -0.18, p = 0.002) and SBP (β = -1.23, p = 0.001). No significant causal associations were observed between napping and fasting glucose or HDL-C. Sensitivity analyses using different MR methods consistently supported the observed associations, suggesting robustness against pleiotropy.

Conclusion:  Our findings provide genetic evidence suggesting a potential causal inverse relationship between daytime napping and BMI and SBP. These results highlight the complex interplay between sleep behavior and cardiometabolic health and warrant further investigation into the underlying biological mechanisms.  Future research should explore the mediating roles of inflammatory markers and metabolic pathways in this relationship.
