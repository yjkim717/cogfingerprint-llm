**Abstract**

The manipulation of photoinduced charge transfer (CT) processes represents a significant challenge in chemical physics, with implications for light-harvesting systems, molecular electronics, and photocatalysis. This work explores the influence of strong light-matter coupling on CT dynamics within a molecular donor-acceptor system embedded in an optical cavity, utilizing a theoretical framework rooted in cavity quantum electrodynamics (cQED). Specifically, we investigate the emergence and impact of polariton-mediated CT, where the hybrid light-matter quasiparticles (polaritons) govern the electron transfer pathway. Employing time-dependent quantum dynamical simulations incorporating ultra-strong coupling regimes (g > ω<sub>0</sub>, where g is the coupling strength and ω<sub>0</sub> is the molecular transition frequency), we demonstrate that the coupling strength and cavity mode structure critically dictate the efficiency and timescale of CT. Our results reveal scenarios where polariton formation enhances CT rates through resonant energy transfer, as well as instances where destructive interference between polariton branches suppresses CT.  Furthermore, we analyze the role of decoherence processes on the polariton dynamics and their impact on the overall CT efficiency. These findings provide fundamental insights into the control of CT via cQED and highlight the potential for engineering molecular systems with tailored photoactive properties through light-matter interaction, paving the way for novel optoelectronic devices.
