**Abstract: Tunable Charge Transfer Dynamics in Molecular Systems via Polariton Engineering within Ultra-Strong Coupling Regimes**

The burgeoning field of cavity quantum electrodynamics (cQED) offers unprecedented opportunities to manipulate and control molecular photochemistry through light-matter interaction. This work investigates the influence of polariton-mediated electron transfer (ET) in donor-acceptor-cavity systems, exploring the potential for both enhancement and suppression of ET rates through strategic cavity design and operating within the ultra-strong coupling regime. Utilizing real-time quantum dynamical simulations incorporating density matrix formalism, we model the dynamics of charge transfer processes in a prototypical organic donor-acceptor complex embedded within a Fabry-Pérot cavity. The simulations demonstrate that, while conventional Förster resonance energy transfer (FRET) is inherently limited by donor decay rates, strong coupling to cavity modes can dramatically alter ET pathways. Specifically, we observe a complex interplay between Rabi oscillations, polariton dispersion, and the system's molecular structure, leading to both accelerated and inhibited ET kinetics dependent on the detuning between the donor emission and the cavity mode frequencies. Furthermore, the emergence of new, polariton-dressed states fundamentally modifies the selection rules governing ET.  Our findings highlight the potential for engineering charge transfer processes with unprecedented control, opening avenues for the development of novel optoelectronic devices and light-harvesting architectures. This work underscores the importance of considering quantum mechanical coherence and light-matter coupling effects in the design of molecular systems for efficient energy transfer.
