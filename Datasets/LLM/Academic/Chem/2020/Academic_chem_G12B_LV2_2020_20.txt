**Abstract**

The manipulation of photoinduced charge transfer (PCT) processes represents a significant challenge in chemical physics, with implications for light-harvesting, photocatalysis, and molecular electronics. This work investigates the influence of strong light-matter coupling on electron transfer kinetics within molecular systems embedded in optical microcavities, leveraging the emergent phenomenon of polariton-mediated interactions. Utilizing a cavity quantum electrodynamics (cQED) framework, we explore the theoretical and experimental considerations surrounding the formation of polaritons – hybrid quasiparticles arising from the coherent superposition of excitonic and photonic states – and their subsequent role in modulating PCT rates. Specifically, we examine how ultra-strong coupling regimes, characterized by Rabi splitting energies exceeding the molecular binding energies, can fundamentally alter the pathways and efficiencies of electron transfer. Our analysis demonstrates that while enhanced light-matter interaction can, in principle, accelerate PCT through polariton condensation and modified electronic structure, competing dephasing mechanisms and cavity losses can conversely suppress transfer rates. This study highlights the complex interplay between coherent quantum effects and dissipative processes in cQED-enhanced PCT, providing insights into the design of novel molecular systems and photonic architectures for controlled light-driven chemical transformations. Further research is warranted to optimize coupling strengths and mitigate detrimental effects to realize efficient polariton-mediated electron transfer.
