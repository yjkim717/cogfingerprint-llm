**Abstract**

The burgeoning field of cavity quantum electrodynamics (cQED) offers unprecedented opportunities to manipulate chemical reactivity through coherent light-matter interactions. This work explores the fundamental role of polariton-mediated electron transfer (PMET) in controlling the dynamics of photoinduced charge transfer (CT) reactions within molecular systems strongly coupled to optical cavities. We investigate how the strength of this coupling, quantified by the Rabi splitting energy, influences the efficiency and pathway of CT processes, moving beyond traditional Marcus theory considerations. Specifically, we examine the interplay between the molecular dipole moment, cavity mode properties, and the resulting polariton dispersion, demonstrating how ultra-strong coupling regimes can fundamentally alter the electronic structure and subsequent CT timescales. Quantum mechanical simulations, employing a density matrix formalism to account for both molecular and photonic degrees of freedom, reveal that PMET can lead to either enhanced or suppressed CT rates depending on the specific system parameters. This sensitivity arises from the renormalization of molecular energy levels and the emergence of new, hybrid light-matter quasiparticles – polaritons – that mediate the electron transfer process. Our findings suggest a departure from the conventional Franck-Condon approximation, highlighting the importance of considering non-adiabatic effects and coherent dynamics in CT within cQED environments. Future research will focus on exploring the application of PMET to catalytic reactions and developing strategies for spatially controlling CT events through the design of tailored cavity geometries and polariton functionalities.
