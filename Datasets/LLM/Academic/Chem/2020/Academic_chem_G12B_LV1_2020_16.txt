**Abstract:**

The burgeoning field of cavity quantum electrodynamics (cQED) offers unprecedented opportunities to manipulate chemical reactivity through light-matter interaction. This work explores the intricate role of polariton-mediated electron transfer (PMET) in photoinduced charge transfer (CT) reactions within molecular systems strongly coupled to optical resonators. We present a theoretical and computational investigation demonstrating that the efficacy of PMET as a reactivity modulator is critically dependent on the relative strength of Hamiltonian terms describing light-matter coupling, molecular electronic structure, and electron-nuclear interactions. Utilizing a multi-configurational time-dependent Hartree-Fock approach coupled with a finite difference time domain solver, we analyze prototypical donor-acceptor dyads exhibiting ultra-strong coupling regimes. Our results reveal that while enhanced CT rates are typically observed under strong coupling conditions, the specific functional form of the cavity-molecule interaction, particularly the balance between bare state and polariton contributions to the overall electronic wavefunction, dictates the ultimate reaction outcome.  Specifically, we identify scenarios where polariton formation can lead to *suppression* of CT, arising from a modified electronic landscape that disfavors charge separation. Furthermore, we highlight the importance of accounting for vibronic degrees of freedom, as these can significantly alter the polariton dispersion and influence the efficiency of CT. These findings provide crucial insights into the design of cQED-enhanced chemical reactions, emphasizing the need for precise control over the light-matter coupling parameters and a comprehensive understanding of the underlying quantum mechanical mechanisms.
