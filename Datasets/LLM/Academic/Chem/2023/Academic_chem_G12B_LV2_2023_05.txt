**Abstract**

The formation of complex organic molecules (COMs) in interstellar environments remains a central challenge in astrochemistry. While gas-phase reactions are understood to contribute, the role of surface chemistry on icy grain mantles is increasingly recognized as crucial, particularly in regions of low temperature and high density. This study investigates a fundamental process underpinning surface-driven COMs: the diffusion of atomic carbon (C) within amorphous astrophysical ices. Using low-temperature scanning tunneling microscopy (STM) and controlled thermal annealing experiments conducted at temperatures ranging from 10 K to 30 K, we have directly observed and quantified the diffusion of individual carbon atoms on a water-ice surface, mimicking conditions prevalent in molecular clouds and cometary nuclei.  Our data allows for the determination of an activation energy (Ea) for this process, measured to be 0.45 ± 0.05 eV. This relatively low activation energy suggests that carbon diffusion can occur readily at interstellar temperatures, facilitating C-C bond formation, a key step in the synthesis of larger organic species. The implications extend to understanding the formation pathways of polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) and other complex refractory organics observed in cometary ejecta and interstellar dust. These findings provide novel constraints for astrochemical models and highlight the importance of considering atomic diffusion mechanisms in the broader context of interstellar organic chemistry.
