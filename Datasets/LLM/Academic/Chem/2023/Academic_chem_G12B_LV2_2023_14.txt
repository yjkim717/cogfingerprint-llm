**Abstract**

The formation of complex organic molecules (COMs) in interstellar environments is a fundamental question in astrochemistry, with surface chemistry on interstellar ice grains widely considered a crucial pathway. While the presence of COMs has been observationally confirmed, the precise mechanisms facilitating their synthesis remain incompletely understood. This study addresses a key rate-limiting step in this process: the surface diffusion of atomic carbon (C) on interstellar ice analogs. Utilizing a novel experimental setup, we have directly measured the diffusion coefficients of C atoms on a water ice surface at cryogenic temperatures (8-15 K) relevant to interstellar conditions. Our results demonstrate a surprisingly efficient surface diffusion of C, with diffusion coefficients exhibiting a temperature dependence consistent with an activation energy of approximately 18 meV. This observation strongly supports the hypothesis that C-C bond formation and subsequent polymerization reactions, leading to the development of larger organic species, are kinetically feasible on interstellar ice surfaces. The measured diffusion rates are significantly faster than previously estimated based on theoretical models, suggesting a potentially enhanced role for C atom surface mobility in the synthesis of COMs. These findings have implications for refining astrophysical models of star and planet formation and provide valuable constraints for laboratory simulations aimed at reproducing interstellar molecular complexity. Further investigation into the influence of ice composition and surface morphology on C atom diffusion is warranted.
