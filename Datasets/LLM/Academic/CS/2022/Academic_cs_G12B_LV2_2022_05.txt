**Abstract**

The increasing prevalence of complex, evolving systems necessitates robust benchmarking methodologies capable of capturing dynamic behavior. This paper presents a novel theoretical framework for dynamic benchmarking, addressing limitations inherent in traditional static assessments. We introduce a formal model incorporating hierarchical dependency structures to represent system components and their interrelationships, allowing for the simulation of emergent behavior and cascading failures.  Our analysis centers on two contrasting models—a discrete-event simulation model and a continuous-time approximation—evaluating their efficacy in representing dynamic system performance under varying operational conditions and levels of label noise introduced during data collection. Simulation results demonstrate that the hierarchical dependency model significantly improves the accuracy of model fitting, particularly in scenarios characterized by high label noise and complex interactions.  However, we also identify limitations associated with computational complexity and the challenges of accurately characterizing the underlying system dynamics.  The findings contribute to a deeper understanding of the trade-offs involved in dynamic benchmarking and provide insights for developing more adaptive and resilient system evaluation techniques. Future work will focus on extending the framework to incorporate real-world data and exploring the applicability of reinforcement learning for adaptive benchmark generation.
