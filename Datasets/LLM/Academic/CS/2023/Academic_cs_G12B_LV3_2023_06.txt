**Abstract: Dynamic Modeling of Risk-Taking and Profitability in the GCC Banking Sector: A Panel Data Approach**

This study investigates the intricate relationship between bank size, market competition, and resultant risk-taking behavior alongside profitability within the Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) banking sector. Utilizing a two-stage Generalized Method of Moments (GMM) estimator on dynamic panel data spanning 2010-2017, we model these interactions, addressing potential endogeneity concerns inherent in observational banking data. Our analysis reveals a nuanced impact of market competition; while heightened competition generally incentivizes efficiency and profitability, excessive competition can simultaneously encourage riskier lending practices in specific bank size categories. We further demonstrate the critical moderating role of bank capital adequacy ratios, finding that robust capital buffers mitigate the adverse effects of intensified competition on risk exposure. The findings underscore the importance of regulatory oversight and capital requirements in fostering a stable and profitable banking environment within the GCC.  This research contributes to the evolving literature on banking stability and provides empirical evidence relevant to policymakers seeking to optimize regulatory frameworks within dynamic financial landscapes, particularly in regions undergoing rapid economic transformation.
