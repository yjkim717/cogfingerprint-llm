**Abstract: Grounding Linguistic Representation in Situated Perception – A Critique of Current NLP Approaches**

Recent advances in Natural Language Processing (NLP) have yielded sophisticated models capable of capturing statistical word similarity. However, a fundamental disconnect persists between these representations and human cognition. This paper argues that prevailing NLP methodologies, largely reliant on distributional semantics, fail to adequately instantiate “meaning” as it is experienced within a framework of embodied perception and action. We posit that current systems lack the crucial grounding derived from human desires, goals, and sensorimotor experiences – a deficiency hindering genuine semantic understanding.  Specifically, we explore how psychological theories of perception and action, particularly situated cognition, offer a contrasting paradigm.  Future research should prioritize integrating multimodal data and incorporating mechanisms for representing relational and contextual information to bridge this representational gap, ultimately moving beyond superficial lexical similarity toward robust, grounded NLP.
