**Abstract**

This paper investigates the revenue-maximizing properties of static pricing strategies within a multi-unit selling environment. While dynamic revenue management techniques are often computationally complex, static pricing remains a practical and widely adopted approach, particularly when dealing with large inventories and limited real-time data. We present a characterization of the revenue efficiency achievable through static pricing schemes, demonstrating that, under certain assumptions regarding customer valuation distributions, a demonstrably optimal pricing threshold exists. Our analysis leverages approximation algorithms to identify this ‘sweet spot’—a price point that balances unit sales volume and individual item valuation to maximize aggregate revenue. Specifically, we explore the trade-off between selling a greater quantity at a lower price versus limiting quantity sold to achieve higher per-unit revenue.  The findings contribute to a deeper understanding of the theoretical limits of static pricing and provide actionable insights for practitioners seeking to optimize revenue generation with simplified pricing models.  The implications extend to diverse applications including e-commerce, resource allocation, and inventory management.
