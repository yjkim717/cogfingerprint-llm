## The Ghost in the Machine: When Online Fun Feels…Off

Remember that initial rush of joy you got from online games? For me, it was *Rocket League*. Hours spent perfecting aerials, celebrating ridiculous goals with teammates, the simple, chaotic thrill of bumping around a giant ball. It was *good*. Pure, unadulterated fun. But lately… something’s shifted.

I’ve noticed it a lot more over the past few months, especially in games like *Rocket League*. You know the feeling – you're genuinely trying to improve, climb the ranks, just enjoy the competition, and then *bam*. You’re matched with someone who’s clearly…not supposed to be there.  They're effortlessly pulling off moves that would take me weeks to master. They're hitting shots from impossible angles. It’s clear they’re a higher-ranked player deliberately playing down in lower divisions – a "smurf," as we call them.

It's not just about losing, though. It’s the feeling of…inauthenticity. The joy of a hard-fought victory feels diminished when you suspect your opponent is deliberately sandbagging. It takes away from the genuine progression of legacy players, those of us who've been grinding for a while.

And it’s a bigger issue than just *Rocket League*.  It's part of a broader trend I've been seeing online.  We’re all craving connection, community, and a sense of shared experience, but the anonymity of the internet can breed behaviors that undermine that. Even something as simple as a cool Loot Llama decal feels less special when it’s a trophy for stomping on players who are just trying to have a good time.

It makes you wonder, doesn’t it? What happens when the pursuit of a competitive edge overshadows the joy of the game itself?  Maybe it’s just the pandemic amplifying existing frustrations, but I miss that initial magic. I miss the feeling of truly earning my place.
